Failure Scenario Analysis (18 mismatches out of 164 glosses, 11.0%)

Of 164 total gloss positions across 53 sentences, 18 mismatches were identified in two root categories: model prediction errors (8 cases) where the correct gloss never appeared in Top-3, and LLM selection errors (10 cases) where the correct gloss was available but the LLM chose incorrectly. Of 53 sentences, 4 (8%) showed CTQI regression: 2 are attributable to gloss-level errors (Categories A and D below), and 2 reflect minor phrasing variations with no gloss mismatch (-3.5 and -1.0 points).

A. Sign Misclassification (8 cases - 44.4% of errors). The recognition model consistently confuses visually similar signs, and the correct sign never appears in the Top-3 candidates - making the error unrecoverable regardless of LLM performance. Example: Signed glosses: LATER, GIVE, JACKET. Model predictions for LATER: [DRINK (45.1%), DEAF, TALL] - LATER never appears. LLM selects DRINK and constructs "I drink and give a jacket" (Plausibility = 100). Should be: "Give the jacket later." The temporal meaning is lost entirely, but the fluent output masks the error.

B. Failed Recovery Despite Error Detection (6 cases - 33.3% of errors). The correct sign appears in Top-3 at extremely low confidence behind a dominant wrong prediction. The wrong candidate produces implausible output, signaling an error - but in 6 of 16 such cases the LLM still fails to select the correct sign. Example: Signed glosses: DOCTOR, NEED, HELP. Model predictions for NEED: [BOWLING (91.5%), TELL (1.1%), NEED (0.8%)]. LLM selects BOWLING and constructs "The doctor is bowling to help" (Plausibility = 7). Should be: "The doctor needs help." The LLM detects the implausibility but cannot recover the correct sign at 0.8% confidence.

C. Plausibility-Driven Misselection (3 cases - 16.7% of errors). The correct sign is available in Top-3, but the dominant wrong candidate produces an equally fluent sentence - giving the LLM no plausibility signal to override the selection. Example: Signed glosses: COMPUTER, PLAY, BASKETBALL. Model predictions for COMPUTER: [SON (67.2%), COMPUTER (20.5%)]. LLM selects SON and constructs "My son plays basketball" (Plausibility = 100). Should be: "Play basketball on the computer." Both sentences are perfectly fluent, so plausibility cannot distinguish correct from incorrect.

D. Semantic Preference Bias (1 case - 5.6% of errors). The correct sign is the model's highest-confidence prediction, but the LLM overrides it in favor of a concrete noun that produces an equally fluent alternative. Example: Signed glosses: CHAIR, FINE, DOCTOR. Model predictions for FINE: [FINE (34.4%), PAPER (23.4%)]. LLM selects PAPER and constructs "The doctor has a chair and paper" (Plausibility = 100). Should be: "The doctor's chair is fine." The LLM prefers the concrete noun PAPER over the adjective FINE despite FINE having higher confidence.

Key Takeaways

1. Model training still has room for improvement: 44.4% of all mismatches are unrecoverable errors where the correct gloss never enters the Top-3 candidate pool. Additional training data for visually confusable signs would eliminate these failures entirely.

2. LLM coherence selection works but has limits: The LLM can override high-confidence wrong predictions when the output is semantically implausible, but fails when the wrong candidate produces an equally fluent sentence — leaving no signal to distinguish correct from incorrect.

3. Plausibility scoring validates the approach: The plausibility component reliably flags semantically broken output while scoring fluent translations highly, confirming its value as an error detection signal.

4. Prompt improvement potential: 10 LLM selection errors affect 6.1% of total glosses. With improved prompting and additional training data, the theoretical ceiling is 0 mismatches in this 53-sentence evaluation set.
