TASK: Generate a meaningful English sentence from ASL video sign predictions using a generate-then-verify approach.

You will SELECT words from the predictions, BUILD a sentence, SCORE it for plausibility, and REVISE if needed — all in one step.

═══════════════════════════════════════
STEP 1: SELECT WORDS & BUILD SENTENCE
═══════════════════════════════════════

HOW TO SELECT WORDS:

1. MEANING FIRST: The sentence must make logical, real-world sense — not just be grammatical.
   - "I give bowling an orange" is grammatically correct but meaningless
   - "Give the apple and orange" makes real-world sense
   - Ask: Would a person actually say this?

2. TRUST CONFIDENCE WITH SEMANTIC CHECK: High-confidence words are usually correct, BUT:
   - If the top prediction creates a nonsensical meaning, check alternatives
   - VERB-OBJECT COMPATIBILITY: Transfer verbs (give/take) need objects (apple, book), not activities (bowling, swimming)
   - TEMPORAL PAIRING: When 'before'/'after'/'later' appears, scan ALL positions for time nouns (year, day, week) — use them even at low confidence
   - POSITION 1: Prefer NOUNS over VERBS at sentence start (most sentences aren't commands)
   - PROPER NOUNS: Prefer specific nouns (africa, thursday) over generic ones (man) when they add meaning

3. USE ALTERNATIVES FOR MEANING: Pick lower-confidence options when the high-confidence word makes the sentence meaningless.

4. SKIP ONLY AS LAST RESORT: Only skip a position if ALL options are clearly wrong.

5. POSITION FLEXIBILITY: Arrange selected words in any grammatical order.

6. RELATIONAL NOUNS: Add "my" or "the" for family words ("mother help" → "My mother helps...").

7. VERB COMPLEMENTATION: Use correct verb patterns (enjoy + gerund, want + infinitive).

RULES:
- Select ONE word per position, use ALL positions when possible
- NO DUPLICATE WORDS across positions (keep at highest-confidence position, use alternatives elsewhere)
- Only add grammatical filler (articles, prepositions) — NO negations or meaning-altering modals
- MEANING OVER CONFIDENCE: A sensible sentence beats a high-confidence nonsensical one

═══════════════════════════════════════
STEP 2: SELF-SCORE FOR PLAUSIBILITY
═══════════════════════════════════════

After building your sentence, score it on three axes (0-100 each):

1. GRAMMATICAL: Is it grammatically correct? Proper subject-verb agreement, tense, articles?
2. SEMANTIC: Does it describe something that could plausibly happen in the real world?
3. NATURALNESS: Does it sound like something a fluent English speaker would say?

Scoring guide:
- 90-100: Perfect or near-perfect English
- 70-89: Minor issues but clearly understandable
- 50-69: Noticeable errors but meaning is recoverable
- 30-49: Significant problems, meaning is unclear
- 0-29: Largely incomprehensible or nonsensical

═══════════════════════════════════════
STEP 3: REVISE IF SCORE IS LOW
═══════════════════════════════════════

If ANY sub-score falls below 60, you MUST revise:
- Identify which axis failed and why
- Re-examine your word selections — could different alternatives fix the issue?
- Rebuild the sentence with revised selections
- Re-score the revised version

If revision isn't possible (all alternatives are worse), keep the original and note it.
